# ETHICS.md — Trialsplained

*Last updated: 8 March 2026*

---

## Why This Document Exists

Trialsplained operates at the intersection of AI, clinical research, and vulnerable people making important health decisions. That combination requires explicit ethical commitments — not as legal cover, but as design constraints.

This document records what we believe, what we've built accordingly, and where we know we haven't got it right yet.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. Do No Harm

The primary obligation of a tool used by people making health decisions is to not make things worse. This means:

- We do not recommend, rank, or endorse any trial
- We do not create urgency ("Only 3 spaces left")
- We do not make participation sound easy when it isn't
- We disclose placebo arms, commitment requirements, and known side effects on every card
- We include withdrawal rights prominently — you can leave any trial at any time

### 2. Informed, Not Persuaded

There is a meaningful difference between helping someone understand their options and nudging them toward a decision. Trialsplained is an information tool, not a recruitment tool.

We have deliberately excluded:
- "Apply now" buttons
- Star ratings or quality rankings
- Testimonials or social proof
- Any language that implies a trial is "right for you"
- Any comparison between trials

The next step after finding a relevant trial is always: speak to your doctor.

### 3. Equity by Default

Health literacy gaps disproportionately affect people from lower socioeconomic backgrounds, older adults, people whose first language is not English, and people with lower formal education. These are also the groups most underrepresented in clinical trials.

Plain-language access is not a nice-to-have. It is the point.

Current measures:
- All summaries written at approximately Year 9 reading level
- Text size and high-contrast accessibility controls
- No jargon in navigation or interface copy
- No assumptions about prior medical knowledge

Known gaps: language translation is not yet implemented. This is a priority for the next version.

### 4. Algorithmic Transparency

Users have a right to know that AI is involved in what they're reading and what that means.

Every trial card includes:
- An AI label: "Plain English summary generated by AI"
- A last-verified date
- A direct link to the official registry listing
- A "Is this accurate?" feedback button

The How It Works page explains the AI process in plain English, including its limitations.

We do not hide the AI layer or present AI summaries as verified medical information.

### 5. Vulnerability-Aware Design

Some users will be using this app in a state of fear, grief, or desperation — a parent who has just received a child's diagnosis, a person facing a condition with no approved treatment, someone who has run out of standard options.

Design decisions made with these users in mind:
- No dark patterns
- No artificial urgency
- No pop-ups or interruptions
- Calm colour palette — no red, no alert colours for trial status
- "You can withdraw at any time" stated on every explainer card
- Disclaimer tone is informative, not alarming

## What Trialsplained Will Never Do

- Collect personal health data
- Require account creation or email registration
- Display advertising
- Accept payment from trial sponsors for placement or visibility
- Rank trials by quality, effectiveness, or desirability
- Claim that any AI summary is medically verified
- Recommend a trial to a specific individual
- Contact users after they leave the site

## What We're Still Working Out

**The simplification problem**  
Making something easier to read can change its meaning. A Phase I safety trial described simply may sound less daunting than it is. We have tried to preserve risk information in all summaries — but we may not always get this right. The feedback mechanism exists in part for this reason.

**The completeness problem**  
We don't list every trial. Our demo covers a limited set of conditions. Users searching for rarer conditions may find nothing, and we don't yet communicate that gap clearly enough.

**The language problem**  
Plain English is only plain English if it's your language. We do not yet offer translation. This is an equity failure we intend to address.

**The currency problem**  
Trial status changes — a recruiting trial can close. Our last-verified dates tell users when a summary was checked, but we cannot guarantee real-time accuracy. Users must always check the official listing.
